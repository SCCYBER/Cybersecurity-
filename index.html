Social engineering is the #1 method hackers use to breach organisations — and it doesn't require any technical skill. It targets people, not computers.

SCENARIO 01 · PHISHING
THE URGENT EMAIL
▸ SITUATION

You get an email marked URGENT from [email protected]. It says your account will be suspended in 2 hours unless you click a link and verify your login details. The email looks professional and uses your company's logo.

▸ WHY THIS MATTERS The email domain your-company-helpdesk.net is NOT your company's real domain — it's a fake. Attackers register lookalike domains to trick you. Legitimate IT teams never ask for credentials via email. Always verify through a known channel — call IT using a number from your company directory, not one in the suspicious email.
TACTIC: Urgency + Impersonation (Phishing)
SCENARIO 02 · VISHING
THE PHONE CALL
▸ SITUATION

You receive a call. The caller says they're from your bank's fraud team. They tell you there's been suspicious activity on your account and they need to verify your identity. They ask you to confirm your full card number and PIN so they can "freeze the account for your protection."

▸ WHY THIS MATTERS Banks never ask for your PIN — ever. Attackers use "vishing" (voice phishing) to create panic. They may already know your name and partial details (from data breaches) to seem legitimate. The only safe move is to hang up and call the official number yourself. Never call back a number they give you.
TACTIC: Vishing + Authority + Urgency
SCENARIO 03 · TAILGATING
THE OPEN DOOR
▸ SITUATION

You badge into your secure office building. As the door closes, someone rushes up behind you — hands full of coffee cups and folders — and says "Oh thank goodness, can you hold that? I left my badge upstairs!" They're wearing a smart outfit and look completely normal.

▸ WHY THIS MATTERS This is called tailgating — one of the most common physical social engineering attacks. Attackers rely on your natural politeness. Looking "normal" is the point. You have no way to verify this person. Politely refusing and directing them to reception is not rude — it's correct procedure. Most legitimate employees understand this.
TACTIC: Tailgating + Politeness Exploitation
SCENARIO 04 · BAITING
THE USB STICK
▸ SITUATION

You find a USB stick in the car park near your office. It has a label that reads "STAFF SALARIES 2024 — CONFIDENTIAL." You're curious and don't recognise it as anything you've seen before.

▸ WHY THIS MATTERS This is called baiting. Attackers deliberately drop USB drives loaded with malware. The label is designed to make you curious. Studies show that 48% of people plug in found USB drives. The moment you connect it, malware can install automatically — on any device, personal or work. Never plug in unknown devices.
TACTIC: Baiting + Curiosity Exploitation
SCENARIO 05 · PRETEXTING
THE NEW COLLEAGUE
▸ SITUATION

Someone messages you on LinkedIn saying they've just joined your company in a different department. They say they're struggling to get set up and ask if you could share the Wi-Fi password for the office, or the link to the internal staff portal. They seem friendly and mention your manager's name.

▸ WHY THIS MATTERS This is pretexting — creating a believable fake identity. LinkedIn is public; anyone can find your manager's name. Wi-Fi access can allow attackers onto your network. Internal portal links reveal your systems' structure. Legitimate new starters get credentials through IT onboarding — they wouldn't need to ask you. Always report these contacts.
TACTIC: Pretexting + Name-Dropping + Trust

▸ THE 5 ATTACKS YOU FACED
  • Phishing — Fake urgent emails designed to steal login credentials. Always verify the sender's domain.
  • Vishing — Phone-based fraud. Banks never ask for PINs. Hang up and call back on official numbers.
  • Tailgating — Physically following someone into a secure area. Politeness is a vulnerability.
  • Baiting — Leaving infected USB drives to exploit curiosity. Never plug in unknown devices.
  • Pretexting — Creating a fake identity to extract information. Legitimate staff use IT onboarding, not social media.
  • The common thread — Every attack targets human instincts: urgency, politeness, curiosity, and trust. Awareness is your best defence.
